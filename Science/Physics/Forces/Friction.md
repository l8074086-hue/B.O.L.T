# Friction

Friction is the force resisting the relative motion of solid surfaces, fluid layers, or material elements sliding against each other.

## Types

### Dry friction
Opposes relative motion of two solid surfaces in contact. Divided into:

- **[[Static friction]]** ($F_s$) — force preventing motion from starting. $F_s \leq \mu_s N$
- **[[Kinetic friction]]** ($F_k$) — force opposing motion already in progress. $F_k = \mu_k N$

Typically $\mu_s > \mu_k$ — it takes more force to start moving than to keep moving.

### Fluid friction
Resistance between layers of fluid moving relative to each other (viscosity). Covers both liquids and gases.

### Rolling friction
Resistance when an object rolls over a surface. Much lower than dry friction — why wheels exist.

### Internal friction
Energy dissipation within a material deforming under stress (hysteresis).

## Key Formula

$$F_f = \mu N$$

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| $F_f$ | Frictional force (N) |
| $\mu$ | [[Coefficient of friction]] (unitless) |
| $N$ | [[Normal force]] (N) |

Friction is **independent of contact area** — depends only on the [[Normal force]] and surface properties.

## Approximate Coefficients

| Surfaces | $\mu_s$ | $\mu_k$ |
|----------|---------|---------|
| Rubber on dry asphalt | ~1.0 | 0.7–0.8 |
| Steel on steel | 0.74 | 0.57 |
| Wood on wood | 0.5 | 0.3 |
| Ice on ice | 0.1 | 0.03 |
| Teflon on steel | 0.04 | 0.04 |

## Useful Models

- **Coulomb friction:** constant $\mu_k$ independent of speed (good approximation for dry surfaces)
- **Viscous friction:** force proportional to velocity ($F \propto v$) — dominates in fluids and lubricated contacts
- **Stribeck curve:** friction drops at low speeds as a lubricant film forms, then rises again with speed
