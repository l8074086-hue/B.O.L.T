# Anotogaster sieboldii — Jumbo Dragonfly

Also known as **golden-ringed dragonfly**, **Siebold's dragonfly**, **Giant spiketail**, *oniyanma* (オニヤンマ), *wúbà gōu tíng* (無霸勾蜓), *jang-su-jamjari* (장수잠자리).

## Taxonomy

| Rank    | Name                          |
| ------- | ----------------------------- |
| Kingdom | Animalia                      |
| Phylum  | Arthropoda                    |
| Class   | Insecta                       |
| Order   | Odonata                       |
| Family  | Cordulegastridae (spiketails) |
| Genus   | *Anotogaster*                 |
| Species | *A. sieboldii* (Sélys, 1854)  |

Subspecies: *A. s. kuchenbeiseri*, *A. s. sieboldii*

## Description

- **Length:** 90–110 mm — largest dragonfly in Japan
- **Eyes:** bright emerald green, meet at a single point on top of the head
- **Body:** black with regular yellow stripes; males and females have nearly identical colouration
- **Wings:** macropterous (fully functional), long pterostigma that does not widen in the centre
- **Sexual dimorphism:** minimal; females have an extremely long ovipositor

The species name *sieboldii* honours Philipp Franz von Siebold. The genus name *Anotogaster* means "belly without ears" (*an* = without, *oto* = ear, *gaster* = belly).

## Distribution

Native to Eastern Asia: **Japan** (Hokkaido to Okinawa), **Korean Peninsula**, **Taiwan**, **China**, eastern **Siberia**. Genetic studies identify at least six distinct populations:

1. Northern Japan mainland + Korea + Yakushima
2. Amamioshima
3. Okinawajima
4. Yaeyama Islands
5. Taiwan
6. East China

The Yaeyama population may be a distinct species (*A. kuchenbeiseri*).

## Habitat

Clean, slow-flowing **small streams**, spring-fed pools, and wetlands near forest edges. Avoids large rivers and lakes. Larvae live buried in sandy mud at the bottom of shallow water.

## Life Cycle

| Stage | Duration | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Egg | ~1 month | Laid in mud/sand under shallow water |
| Nymph (larva) | 3–5 years | ~10 moults, grows to 5 cm; overwinters as larva |
| Adult | 1–2 months | Emerges June–September |

- **Oviposition:** Females hover vertically above water and stab their ovipositor repeatedly into soft mud/sand. Males may grab females mid-air to mate; copulation can last over 2 hours.
- **Emergence:** Nymphs crawl out at night onto rocks or banks to shed their exoskeleton, emerging as winged adults by morning.
- **Patrolling:** Mature males patrol fixed routes along streams (~2 m/s, 20 cm above water). They perch hanging from vegetation, not resting horizontally.

## Diet

- **Adults:** moths, flies, horseflies, wasps — caught mid-flight. Powerful mandibles; bites are painful.
- **Larvae:** tadpoles, aquatic insects, small fish.

## Behaviour

- Freshly emerged adults gather in open sunny areas away from water, sometimes forming loose swarms.
- Body temperature during flight: ~40°C.
- Flight speed decreases in higher temperatures; flight altitude drops when cooler.
- Very cautious and difficult to approach.

## Conservation

- **IUCN:** Least Concern
- **Tokyo:** Near Threatened — vulnerable due to development of small streams and groundwater pollution.

## Cultural Notes

- The Japanese name *oniyanma* (鬼蜻蜓) means "demon dragonfly."
- Both adults and larvae were historically eaten: adults skewered and grilled with soy sauce; larvae dry-roasted with salt or boiled with small fish.
- A common late-summer sight in Japan.
